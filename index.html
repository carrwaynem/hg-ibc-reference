Unit 1
Foundations

The big idea behind Infinite Banking, told in simple words.

What is Infinite Banking (IBC)?
Infinite Banking means using a whole life insurance policy as your own personal bank. Not any whole life policy: one that has been specially designed and engineered to do this job. When you need to buy something big, you borrow from your own policy instead of a bank, so the interest you pay goes back to you, not a stranger.

But buying something big is only part of it. So is paying down debt, and so is simply being a better steward of your money. The deeper reason is that a policy built this way is a better place to keep your money than a bank. It turns your cash into a second stream of income that works very passively in the background, because it earns a dividend whether you ever borrow from it or not. In Hidden Gems Financial's own words: "Infinite Banking is not a product you buy; it is a strategy you build."
Mutual insurance companyVerified
A company owned by the people who buy policies from it, not by outside investors. There's no stock to trade. So when the company makes extra money, it doesn't go to rich stockholders. It comes back to policyholders like you as a dividend, and that dividend is what accelerates your cash value growth. That is the engine driving your cash value up year after year.
Participating whole lifeVerified
A whole life policy that gets to share in the company's profits. It also comes with three promises: the same payment every year, cash value that always grows, and money paid to your family when you die.
Divisible surplusVerified
This is the extra money (profit) left over at the end of the year. Company leaders decide how much to save for a rainy day and how much to hand back to policyholders. The part handed back is what becomes your dividend. That extra money usually comes from four places:
Investments did better than expected
Fewer people passed away than expected
The company spent less running things than expected
Fewer people canceled their policy than expected
The Contribution PrincipleVerified
The rule these companies use to split the profit fairly: each policy gets back a share that matches how much it helped create that profit. That's also why two different companies might pay your dividend a little differently if you have a loan out.
Dividends: not a promise, and not taxed like stockVerified
A dividend is never a sure thing. It depends on how well the company did that year. But it's different from a stock dividend: instead of being a slice of someone else's profit, it's treated like getting back a little of the extra money you already paid in. That's why you usually don't pay tax on it. Many of these companies have paid a dividend every single year for over 120 years, through wars, the Great Depression, and the 2008 crash.
Unit 2
Becoming Your Own Banker

Simple stories from R. Nelson Nash's book that explain why this whole idea works. Told in our own words. No purchase links here.

The grocery store storyVerified
Picture your family owns a grocery store, and you shop there too. If your family sneaks food out the back door without paying, they're stealing from their own store! The store only makes money if everyone, even family, pays at the front register. A funded policy works the same way. Paying your policy back isn't just a rule to follow. It's what keeps your own little bank healthy and growing. Skip paying it back, and it's like sneaking food out the back door of your own store.
The headwind / tailwind problemVerified
Nash figured that most families send about a third of every extra dollar toward interest on cars, houses, and credit cards, while saving very little. He compares this to flying a plane straight into a strong headwind: you can't win by just gunning the engine harder (saving a tiny bit more). The bigger win is changing the wind itself, taking control of your own money system, and turning that headwind into a tailwind pushing you forward.
The shortcut to having your own bankVerified
Starting a real, official bank costs tens of millions of dollars and years of government paperwork. Almost nobody does that. Nash compares it to a paper mill: instead of building a whole new power plant, they burn their own leftover wood scraps to make electricity, then plug straight into the power lines that already exist. A whole life policy lets you do the same thing: plug into a banking system that's already been running for 200 years, without ever starting a real bank.
You always get first pick of your own moneyVerified
In your policy, you are called the Owner. That means you get first pick of any money that can be borrowed from it, up to 100% of what it's worth, before anyone else. In plain words: nobody else's loan ever gets in the way of you using your own cash value.
Why starting a real bank is so much harderVerified
A real bank can only lend out money that people put in. It can't lend money it doesn't have. If too many people stop paying back their loans, the bank can run out of money fast and collapse. That really happened to a bank in Texas back in the 1980s. A well-built whole life policy doesn't work that way. Your promises are written into your own contract. They don't depend on other people trusting the same bank.

Three approaches to alleviate debtCompany material
There are three ways people go after debt. They are not a ladder you climb in order. They are three different tools, and the third one makes the first two work harder.
The snowball method
List your debts from smallest to biggest. Pay extra on the smallest one while paying the minimum on the rest. When it's gone, roll that payment onto the next one. You feel a win early, and that keeps you going.
The avalanche method
List your debts from the highest interest rate down to the lowest. Put your extra money toward the highest-rate debt first. This saves you the most money over time.
Infinite Banking
Borrow from your own policy to clear the debt, then pay yourself back. The interest goes to you instead of a lender, and your cash value keeps earning the entire time.
The hybrid, where it gets good: you don't have to pick just one. Infinite Banking supercharges both of the others. Run a snowball or run an avalanche, but fund it with a policy loan instead of a lender's money. The debts still fall in the same order. The difference is that the interest you were going to pay anyway now lands back in your own policy, and that policy keeps growing the whole time it's doing the work.

Where the policy fits in your monthly budgetCompany material

Same expenses, same budget, but now your money does a second job on the way. And since you're paying that card from your policy anyway, you can send more than the balance and start eating into debt that was already sitting there.
Unit 4
Policy Anatomy

The pieces that make up your policy.

Base premium
The smallest payment you're allowed to make to keep your policy's promises, the death benefit and the cash value growth, going strong.
Paid-up additions (PUA)
Extra payments you can choose to make that buy small bits of paid-off whole life insurance. This is the best way to make your cash value grow faster.
Net cash value
The amount of money in your policy that's really yours to use right now: the full cash value minus anything you still owe on a loan.
Death benefit
The money paid to your family when you pass away, usually with no tax owed on it. It gives your family instant money, instead of them having to wait years for savings to build up.
1035 exchangeVerified
A way to trade your whole life insurance or annuity contract for a new one without paying tax, not just the cash value but the whole thing, as long as the same person owns both. This lets you switch to a better policy without a tax bill.
Unit 5
Core Mechanics

How money moves in and out of your policy.

Borrow vs. withdrawal
A loan means you borrow money using your cash value as collateral. Your cash value keeps earning dividends the whole time, and you pay the loan back whenever you want. A withdrawal actually takes money out of your cash value for good, so it earns less going forward (and it might shrink the death benefit too).
Interest vs. dividends
Interest is what you pay the company when you borrow from your policy. Dividends are what the company pays you for being a policyholder. In a healthy policy, both can happen at the same time: you pay interest on a loan and earn dividends at once.
Non-direct recognition
The company pays you dividends on your whole cash value, whether or not you have a loan out. The loan and the dividend aren't connected to each other.
Direct recognition
The company changes the dividend on the part of your cash value you've borrowed against, often paying it a different rate. Neither way is "better." They're just two different ways of keeping things fair between people who borrow and people who don't.
Unit 6
The Principles Behind It

Big ideas from R. Nelson Nash and other IBC teachers that explain why this works, not just how.

Opportunity costVerified
You pay to finance everything you buy: either you pay interest to someone else, or you give up interest you could've earned by keeping your own money working. Paying with cash is never really "free"; you just gave up what that money could have done for you somewhere else.
The broken window story: a boy breaks a shop window. Everyone says "good, now the window fixer gets paid!" That's what you can see. What you can't see: the shopkeeper would've spent that money on new shoes instead. Nothing new was made. The money just went somewhere else, and now the shopkeeper is missing a window too. (This story is almost 200 years old and people still use it to explain opportunity cost.)
The Golden Rule
"Whoever has the gold makes the rules." Whoever controls the money in a deal controls the terms. IBC is about putting yourself in that spot for your own money, instead of a bank.
Willie Sutton's Law
Named after a famous bank robber. When asked why he robbed banks, he said, "because that's where the money is." Nash's point: go to where the money and the banking actually happen.
Parkinson's Law
Spending grows to match how much you earn. The more money you make, the more you tend to spend, unless you build something on purpose (like a funded policy) that grabs the extra money first.
The Arrival Syndrome
The mistaken idea that once you reach a goal (paying off your house, hitting a savings number), you can stop thinking carefully about money. IBC treats banking as something you always do, not a finish line.
Start-up cost: an IUL problem, not a whole life one
An IUL carries a significant start-up cost, and it takes roughly 13 years to earn it back. The fees are front-loaded, meaning they're packed into the early years. That opens a gap between your cash value (the number on the statement) and your surrender value (what you could actually walk away with), and closing that gap takes about 13 years.

This is exactly why an IUL cannot do infinite banking. If you have to wait 13 years for the thing to break even, the word "infinite" no longer applies. A bank you can't use for over a decade isn't a bank. A properly built whole life policy has no such ramp-up period: no early start-up cost to climb out of, and no gap between cash value and surrender value to wait out.
Use it or lose it
Your own money has a cost too, even sitting still, just like borrowed money does. Nash's point: thinking your own money is "free" just because you already have it is the same mistake as ignoring opportunity cost.
The four roles in any money system
Nash's way of showing who earns what: the Stockholder earns dividends, the Saver earns interest, the Worker earns a paycheck, and the Borrower pays interest. A person with a mutual whole life policy is unusual: they can be the dividend-earner and the borrower at the same time.
Smart risk vs. risky risk
Smart risk means only risking money you can afford to lose, after your safety net (guaranteed cash value, protection) is already in place. Risky risk means gambling with the very money that was supposed to protect you.
Get your net worth now, don't wait for it
A death benefit gives your family a big chunk of money right away. Instead of spending decades slowly saving up to that number, the policy creates it instantly.
Using your money beats just stacking it up
A good policy's real job is to protect your money, help it grow, and let you use and enjoy it today and tomorrow, instead of locking it away untouched for decades.
Unit 7
What It's Not

Things people mix up with IBC, or try to swap in, that just don't do the same job.

Whole life vs. IUL: four big promisesVerified
WhatWhole LifeIUL
PaymentSame every time, promisedCan change
Cost of insuranceStays the same foreverGoes up as you age
Cash value growthPromised in the contractNot promised, can be $0
Who can change the deal laterNobody, it's a contractThe insurance company can
That last row is the whole point. Your own "bank" has to be something nobody else can change the rules on after you've put your money in. With IUL, the company can change some of the numbers later. With whole life, they can't. It's locked in.
Cap, floor, and participation rate
These are IUL words: a cap is the most you can earn, no matter how well the stock market does. A floor is how low your earnings can go (often 0%, so at least you don't lose money). A participation rate is how much of the market's gain you actually get. None of these numbers are locked in. The company can change them.
Annual renewable term (ART)
This is the type of insurance cost hiding inside most IUL and universal life policies. The price goes up every single year as you get older, slowly eating away at your cash value just to keep your coverage going.
The three sets of numbers on every IUL
An IUL illustration doesn't show you one set of numbers. It shows three, and only the first is an actual promise.
1. The guarantee
The only column the company is truly bound to. It often shows the policy running out of money early.
2. The midpoint
A middle-of-the-road projection sitting between the guarantee and the top number. Also not promised.
3. "Current assumptions"
Built from the last 20 years of performance. This is a number the insurance company creates to illustrate the most they're allowed to project, usually somewhere between 6% and 7%.
That third column is the one people walk away remembering, and it's the one nobody promised. The gap between column one and column three is a risk whole life simply doesn't have.
Read your illustration
You don't have to take anyone's word for any of this, including ours. If you already own a policy, go get your illustration and find the page called Numeric Summary or Tabular Detail. That's where the three columns live. Then answer three questions about your own contract:
1. Does it ever run out?
In the guaranteed column, find the age where your cash value hits zero. Write down that age.
2. What rate is it assuming?
Find the rate the current assumptions column is projecting. It's usually printed at the top of the column.
3. How far apart are they?
At age 65, compare the guaranteed column to the current assumptions column. That distance is the part nobody promised you.
Whatever you find, you found it yourself, in your own paperwork. That's the whole point. If the two columns tell the same story, you're in good shape. If they don't, now you know which one you were sold on.

Why real banks buy whole life tooVerified
Banks themselves buy billions of dollars of whole life insurance to hold onto as a safe, steady asset. It's used to help pay for things like employee benefits. Regulators trust it because insurance companies are careful, steady lenders, not risky like banks can be. If whole life is safe enough for an actual bank to use, that's a good sign it's the right tool for building your own.
Watch out for tricky wordsVerified
"Permanent life insurance" and "cash value life insurance" are real terms, but they're umbrella words that cover four different products, only one of which is whole life. A policy can be sold using those broad words without ever saying "whole life" or "guaranteed," so a buyer might think they're getting promises they're not actually getting. State regulators have warned people about exactly this: buying a "permanent" policy expecting a locked-in payment and guaranteed growth, then later finding out costs went up or the policy is running out of money. If someone sells you a policy for infinite banking, make sure they say the word "whole life" and use the word "guaranteed," not just "permanent" or "cash value."
Try it: spot the red flag
Someone tells you a policy is "permanent cash value life insurance" for infinite banking, but never says "whole life" or "guaranteed." What should you ask?
Nothing. "Permanent" already means it's guaranteed
"Is this whole life? Are the payment, the cost, and the cash value growth all guaranteed?"
Only ask how much money my family gets when I die
Unit 8
Protect the Structure

Rules that protect your tax benefits and protect your family if you can't pay anymore.

MEC (putting in money too fast)Verified
If you put money into a policy too quickly, it turns into something called a MEC. Your family still gets the death benefit tax-free. But now, if you borrow or take money out early, you have to pay tax on it (and maybe an extra penalty if you're under 59½).
Term rider
Extra insurance added on top of your whole life policy. It increases the benefit your family receives, and it's also there to satisfy a tax rule called 7702. That rule governs how much insurance you're required to carry in order to move a given amount of money through a policy. So the amount of money you're using to run the policy is what determines how big the term rider needs to be. It typically lasts for the entire funding period, because that's what allows those capital amounts to make it into the policy at all.
Waiver of premium
A add-on that keeps your policy fully paid for you, no payments needed, if you become too disabled to work and pay.
Living benefitsStandard
These let you use some of your death benefit money while you're still alive, if you get seriously sick. They're included as standard, so this isn't something extra you have to go out and buy.
Two ways to stop paying (often mixed up)
Reduced paid-up: your death benefit gets smaller forever, but the policy keeps going for your whole life, still earning dividends and growing, and you never pay again. Extended term: your family still gets the full death benefit, but only for a set number of years, and your cash value stops growing.
Fine print: borrowing from your policy
If you don't pay back the interest on a loan, it gets added to what you owe, and it shrinks the death benefit. If your loan ever grows bigger than your cash value, your whole policy could end, and the loan might even become taxable.
Skipping the long court process (probate)
When you name a real person as your beneficiary, the death benefit usually skips a long court process called probate completely. It goes to your family faster and more privately than most other things you own.
Unit 9
Retirement Accounts & OPM

How a policy compares to (and can team up with) accounts like a 401(k) or IRA.

RMD: the government makes you take money outVerified
Starting at age 73 or 75 (it depends on your birth year), the government makes you start pulling money out of most retirement accounts, whether you need it or not, and you pay regular tax on it. A Roth IRA doesn't have this rule for the original owner.
60-day rule
If you pull money straight out of a retirement account, you have 60 days to put it back into a similar account, or the government treats it as regular taxable income.
OPM: using other people's money
Instead of always selling your own investments to pay for something, you borrow from your policy (or elsewhere) so your investments can keep growing untouched.
Unit 10
Apply It in Real Life

Real examples of how this actually gets used.

Paying off a credit card the smart way
Say you owe $4,000 on a credit card at 22% interest. A policy loan might only cost 7–8% interest. Paying off the card with a policy loan cuts your interest cost roughly in half, and the interest you do pay goes back into your own policy, not the credit card company.
Getting a better deal on a car
Say a car costs $30,000 at 9% interest from the dealer. Using your cash value to pay for it (or lower the rate) sends that interest back to your own policy instead of the dealer's lender, while your cash value keeps earning dividends the whole time.
Setting a clear goal to pay off debtCompany material
An example from Hidden Gems Financial's own guide: pay off $8,000 in credit card debt, by paying $700 extra a month, by cutting $350 from spending and earning $350 more from a side job, so you're debt-free in 12 months. A clear goal like this works. A vague goal like "save more money" usually doesn't.
Now add the policy, and watch it accelerate: run that same $700 a month through your policy and use a policy loan to clear the card. The debt still goes away, but two things change. The interest you were handing the card company now comes back to you instead. And when the 12 months are up, you're not just debt-free: the cash value you built along the way is still sitting there, still earning. That's the difference between paying off debt and paying off debt with an asset to show for it.
Unit 11
Who It's For

Who this really helps, and an honest look at who it doesn't.

People ready to go past "just getting out of debt"Company material
Most people stop once their debt is paid off. IBC is for someone who already has (or is building) a safety net, and now wants their extra money to start working for them, instead of just no longer working against them.
Anyone tired of paying interest to everyone else
If a big chunk of your money already goes toward interest on cars, cards, or a house, IBC is a way to send that same money back to yourself instead. You were paying it either way.
Small business owners
People who want quick, promised access to money for supplies, equipment, or slow months, without waiting on a bank to say yes.
Families who care about more than just retirement
People who want one guaranteed part of their money plan that doesn't depend on the stock market, and who want money to reach their kids fast and privately, without a long court process.
Who it's probably not for
This isn't about having extra money lying around. You don't need to find new dollars, because we're reallocating the ones already moving through your life. What it does ask is that you're willing to steward your money on purpose: to know where it's going, and to route it through the policy instead of past it. If you're not ready to do that part, the structure can't do its job.

It's also not for anyone chasing the fastest possible growth. Whole life is meant to be slow, safe, and boring, not a way to get rich quick. And policy design really matters, so this should always be a conversation with someone qualified, not a do-it-yourself decision.
Unit 12
Common Questions

Real questions people actually ask on their first call, pulled from real conversations, with names left out.

Why not just put my money in the stock market instead?
Stocks can grow faster some years, but they go up and down, and borrowing against them can get risky. If the value drops, a lender can force you to sell at the worst possible time. A whole life policy grows slower but steadier, and nobody can force you to sell or repay on their schedule.
Why not just buy cheap term insurance and invest the rest myself?
Term insurance is cheaper and pays your family if you die, but it never builds any cash value. When the term ends, you have nothing left from it. A whole life policy costs more, but part of every payment builds real cash you can use while you're still alive.
Isn't most of my first payment just going to fees?Verified
Not with real whole life. The cost of insurance is a small, level slice of every payment, the same amount on day one as it is 30 years later, so it never quietly grows into a bigger piece of your payment. Most well-built whole life policies give you access to a strong majority of what you've paid in within about 30 days of that very first payment. And your cash value doesn't just "catch up" later. It starts strong and gets a little stronger every single year, since nothing is eating into it from a rising insurance cost.

That "small at first, catches up later" pattern is actually how IUL works, not whole life. IUL policies charge a separate, rising cost of insurance and a steep surrender charge, so the cash you could actually walk away with in year one can be a small fraction of what you paid in, sometimes barely a dime on the dollar. Whole life doesn't work that way.
If my loan costs more than my dividend pays, isn't that a bad deal?
Look closely at what each rate is charged on. You pay interest only on the piece you borrowed. You earn a dividend on the entire policy. Those are two very different sized buckets, and that's what makes this work.
Run the numbers: say you have a $100,000 policy and you borrow $20,000. You pay 7%, but only on the $20,000 you took, which is $1,400. You earn 5%, but on the full $100,000, which is $5,000. The higher rate is sitting on the smaller number. You still come out ahead.
This matters most in the early years, when you're unlikely to be pulling money out anyway. And the rates are only half the story. What really decides the outcome is what you do with the borrowed money. Are you buying down a higher interest rate somewhere else while your own money stays invested and keeps earning? Are you putting it toward something that generates revenue? Whatever those dollars earn out in the world comes back to offset the gap between your dividend and the interest you're charged.
What happens if I never pay back a loan from my policy?
You're never on a set schedule to pay back a policy loan. But if you skip the interest, it gets added to what you owe. If a loan ever grows bigger than your cash value, the policy could lapse, and that can create a tax bill. Paying at least the interest each year keeps that from happening.
This sounds too good to be true. What's the catch?
The real "catch" is patience: whole life grows slowly at first and works best when you fund it consistently for years. It's not a secret loophole. It's a 200-year-old, heavily regulated product. The "magic" is really just steady compounding, plus not needing a bank's permission to use your own money.
I've heard whole life insurance has a bad reputation. Is that fair?
That reputation usually traces back to a different style of whole life: one that's mostly death benefit with very little cash value growth. It isn't outdated, and it isn't bad. It's simply built for a different job, and it's still sold today for exactly that reason. Regular whole life and infinite banking whole life live side by side, the same way both approaches coexist right now.

A policy built on purpose for infinite banking is engineered differently. Paid-up additions are layered in specifically to make the cash value grow much faster. Same product family, different design. And the design matters as much as the product itself.
Is this the same as an IUL (Indexed Universal Life)?
No. Mixing these two up is one of the most common mistakes. An IUL ties your growth to the stock market with a cap on your gains, and its cost of insurance rises every year as you age. Whole life has neither problem: the cost of insurance never changes, and growth isn't tied to the market at all.
How fast can I actually get my money if I need it?
In most well-funded policies, a large share of your money (often 70–90%, depending on the company) is available to borrow within about 2 to 4 weeks, much faster than a lot of other assets, and nobody has to approve it but you.
Is my money safer with an insurance company than in a bank?
Insurance companies are required to hold on to far more of their money in reserve than banks are. That's a big part of why many mutual insurers have paid a dividend every single year for over 120 years, through the Great Depression, wars, and market crashes, without missing one.
I'm only a few years from retirement. Is it too late to start?
There's no early start-up cost to climb out of, so that isn't the thing to weigh here. What actually changes as you get older is the cost of the insurance itself: it's higher. That means it takes a larger contribution to make a real impact. That's the honest tradeoff, and it's a very different question than "is it too late."

For the right person, it still makes sense this close to retirement. One route is funding the policy from a 401(k): the 401(k) keeps doing its job of deferring taxes, while the policy gives you tax-free access to your cash and a tax-free legacy for your family. There are several ways to approach it, which is exactly why this is a conversation rather than a yes-or-no answer.
